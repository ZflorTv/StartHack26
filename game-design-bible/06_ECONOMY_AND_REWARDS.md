# Economy & Rewards

> *Flowers buy plants, smart decisions earn flowers, and every point on the score tells a story about the player's financial instincts.*

---

## 🌟 The Fantasy

The player is managing two things at once: their garden's health and their flower budget. Flowers aren't just currency — they're **proof of good judgment**. A player with 2,000 flowers didn't just play the game. They played it well. They held when others panicked. They diversified when others concentrated. Every flower is a tiny trophy.

## 🔄 The Loop

### The Flower Economy

**Flowers** are Growden's single in-game currency, and their flow is elegantly simple:

**How You Earn Flowers:**
| Source | Amount | What It Teaches |
|--------|--------|----------------|
| Starting balance | 1,000 | Everyone begins equal |
| Level completion | +200 | Persistence is rewarded |
| Holding during an event | +15 | Patience pays |
| Diversification bonus | +20 per level | Spread your bets |

**How You Lose Flowers:**
| Action | Penalty | What It Teaches |
|--------|---------|----------------|
| Panic selling | -25 | Emotional decisions cost money |
| Overtrading in calm | -10 | Doing nothing IS a strategy |
| ESG violation (Coal Bush) | -10 | Shortcuts have long-term costs |

**How You Spend Flowers:**
- Each new plant costs **100 flowers**
- Maximum garden size: **10 plants**
- Total possible plant investment: 1,000 flowers

The math is intentional. A player who earns every bonus and avoids every penalty accumulates roughly 3,000+ flowers across 10 levels — enough to buy every plant they unlock and still feel wealthy. A player who panic-sells repeatedly might end up with only 1,200 flowers, forcing them to make painful choices about which plants to buy. **The economy punishes bad behavior through scarcity, not game-over screens.**

### The Scoring System

Score operates separately from flowers — it's the player's **reputation**, not their wallet:

**Positive Score Actions:**
- Hold decision during event: +15 points
- Rebalance decision: +5 points
- Diversification bonus: +20 per level (if portfolio spread is healthy)
- Level completion: +10 per level
- Growth bonus: +50 (if portfolio grows 50%+ from start)

**Negative Score Actions:**
- Panic sell: -25 points (or **-50 during Market Panic** — double penalty)
- Overtrading: -10 points
- ESG violation: -10 points
- Leveraged Vine liquidation: -35 points
- Holding bad plants through shocks: -20 points

The separation of flowers and score creates **two distinct feedback channels**. Flowers tell the player "how much can I do?" Score tells them "how well did I do it?" A player can have high flowers (they earned a lot) but a low score (they also made costly mistakes). This mirrors real finance, where portfolio size and portfolio quality are different conversations.

## 🎲 The Tension & Drama

The economic tension lives in the **spend decision**. Every plant purchase is irreversible and costs 100 flowers. But more plants means more diversification, which means higher diversification bonuses, which means more flowers. **Spending wisely creates a virtuous cycle. Spending poorly creates a vicious one.**

The most dramatic economic moment comes at **levels 6–8** when bad plants unlock. Junk Weed offers 8% yield — genuinely attractive. But if it defaults, the player loses the plant AND eats a massive score penalty. Leveraged Vine amplifies returns 5x — but liquidation means -35 points and the plant vanishes from the garden forever.

The economy doesn't tell players "don't buy these." It lets them buy, experience the consequences, and learn why high yield and high leverage carry hidden costs. **The price of wisdom is paid in flowers.**

## 🧠 The Psychology

- **Loss Aversion in Action** — Losing 25 flowers to a panic sell feels worse than gaining 15 for a hold feels good. The asymmetry is intentional — it mirrors the psychological reality of financial losses, which studies show hurt roughly twice as much as equivalent gains feel good.

- **Earned vs. Given** — Flowers aren't free. They come from specific good decisions. This makes spending them feel weightier — the player isn't spending "game money," they're spending proof of their competence.

- **Visible Wealth** — The flower counter is always visible in the HUD. A high count is a constant pat on the back. A low count is a constant reminder to play smarter.

- **The Sunk Cost Trap** — After buying Coal Bush for 100 flowers, players are reluctant to sell it even when they realize it's dangerous. "But I paid for it!" This is the sunk cost fallacy — and the game lets players discover it firsthand.

## 💰 The Business Connection

The economy serves three business functions:

**1. Pacing Mechanism:** Flower costs prevent players from buying all plants immediately. Unlocking + affording plants creates a double-gated progression that extends engagement.

**2. Behavioral Scorecard:** The scoring system creates a quantified measure of financial decision-making quality. For PostFinance, this data (in aggregate) demonstrates educational outcomes: "Players who completed 3 playthroughs improved their average score by 40%."

**3. Monetization Foundation:** While the current build has no monetization, the flower economy is perfectly structured for it. Premium flower packs, exclusive plant skins, or "Flora's Insurance" (undo one panic sell) are natural, non-predatory revenue streams that don't undermine the educational mission.

**Critical Design Integrity:** The economy cannot let players buy score. Score must always reflect decisions, not spending. This ensures the educational credibility of the product. Flowers can unlock options; only choices can earn points.

## ⚠️ Design Gaps & Open Questions

- ⚠️ **Design Gap** — *Flowers have no use after all plants are purchased. Late-game players accumulate flowers with nothing to spend them on. Cosmetic upgrades, garden decorations, or charity donations (tie to real-world PostFinance ESG initiatives?) could solve this.*
- ⚠️ **Design Gap** — *The penalty/reward ratio (-25 for panic sell vs. +15 for hold) is aggressive. Is this calibrated to feel fair, or does it feel punishing? Player testing needed to find the sweet spot between consequence and frustration.*
- ⚠️ **Design Gap** — *No inflation mechanic. Plant costs are static at 100 flowers regardless of level. Should later plants cost more, teaching that asset prices rise over time?*

## 🔮 Design Opportunities

1. **Flower Dividends:** Plants that pay small flower dividends each level — teaching passive income. Bond bushes pay 5 flowers/level. Equity trees pay 3 but grow in value. Crypto orchids pay nothing but might 10x. The portfolio starts generating its own income.

2. **Insurance Market:** Let players spend flowers to "insure" plants against specific events. 50 flowers to protect your Cherry Blossom from the next storm. This teaches options and hedging — advanced concepts made tangible through the garden metaphor.

3. **Generosity Bonus:** Let players gift flowers to other players (when multiplayer launches). Generosity could unlock exclusive achievements or plant variants — teaching that financial success isn't zero-sum.
