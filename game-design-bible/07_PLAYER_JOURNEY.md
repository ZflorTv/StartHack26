# Player Journey

> *From "what is this?" to "I understand investing" — the emotional arc of a player who came for a game and left with a financial education.*

---

## 🌟 The Fantasy

The journey is one of transformation. The player arrives thinking investing is scary, complicated, and for other people. They leave thinking it's a garden — something you tend with patience, diversify with intention, and protect from storms with preparation. The fantasy is **becoming someone who gets it**.

## 🔄 The Loop

### Minute 0–1: First Impressions

The player opens Growden and sees a garden. Not a trading terminal. Not a chart. A garden with gently swaying plants and floating particles. The PostFinance branding is present but subtle. The feeling is: *this is calm, this is beautiful, this might be fun.*

They enter their name and hit "Start Growing."

### Minute 1–3: "Who Am I?"

The game asks the first strategic question: **Choose your gardening style.**

- 🧘 **Zen Garden** — "I like things calm and steady"
- 🌿 **Meadow** — "I like a bit of everything"
- 🌴 **Jungle** — "I like things wild and exciting"

The player doesn't realize they're selecting a risk profile. They're choosing a personality. This is the game's first act of translation — turning financial jargon into human identity.

### Minute 3–5: The Tutorial

Six quick steps, each with a single idea:

1. "Your garden is your portfolio" — the core metaphor
2. "Each plant is a different type of investment" — the asset class mapping
3. "Weather is the market" — events create consequences
4. "Mix your plants" — diversification saves you
5. "Flowers are your budget" — resource management
6. "Stay calm during storms" — the most important lesson

The tutorial takes under two minutes. It's designed to plant seeds (pun intended) that bloom through gameplay.

### Minute 5–8: "Oh No, Weather!"

Level 1 begins. The player has five starter plants. The first event hits — Earnings Season. The sun comes out, trees grow, the player feels good. Score goes up.

Then a Calm Day. Nothing happens. If the player trades (because they feel like they should "do something"), they lose points. First lesson learned: **sometimes the best move is no move.**

Level 2 introduces a Fed Rate Hike. Rain falls. Bonds drop. Cash gains. The player sees their Camellia bush shrink and their Meadow Grass grow. They're confused — but intrigued. "Wait, cash goes UP when rates go up?"

**This is the moment the game earns the player's attention.** They're not just clicking buttons. They're learning something.

### Minute 8–12: The Middle Act

Levels 3–6 are the learning crucible:

- **Level 3:** An Unemployment Spike hits. The player's trees crumble. Their bonds rally. For the first time, they're grateful they diversified. The diversification bonus flashes: +20 points. The game rewards what it teaches.

- **Level 5:** Inflation Shock. Acid rain corrodes the cash and bonds. But the Golden Barrel cactus thrives. The player learns that the "boring" asset they almost skipped is actually insurance against a specific kind of disaster.

- **Level 6:** **Junk Weed unlocks.** It looks like a bond. It offers high yield. The player buys it. Three events later, it defaults. -25 points. The plant dies. The player stares at their screen. They just learned more about credit risk in 10 seconds than most adults learn in a lifetime.

### Minute 12–15: The Crisis

Levels 7–9 are the emotional climax:

- **Level 7:** Market Panic. Tornado animation. Portfolio down 25%. The game presents three buttons: Hold, Rebalance, Panic Sell. The panic sell button is right there. It's tempting. The player's finger hovers.

  If they hold: +25 points (special Market Panic bonus). The storm passes. Their garden recovers.
  If they panic sell: -50 points (double penalty). The garden stabilizes — but at a permanent loss.

  **This is the game's defining moment.** Every player remembers their first Market Panic decision.

- **Level 9:** Sector Mania. Crypto explodes +80%. The player feels euphoric. Their orchids are glowing. Then the rebalance window appears. "Do you want to adjust your garden?" Most players don't — why would they sell something that just went up 80%?

  Phase 2 hits. Crypto crashes -75%. The orchids wilt to nothing. The players who rebalanced are smiling. Everyone else is learning about FOMO.

### Minute 15: The Debrief

Level 10 ends. The game shows a comprehensive debrief:

- Final score and tier (Seedling through Master Gardener)
- Portfolio growth percentage
- Key moments replayed (best holds, worst panic sells)
- Achievements earned (confetti animations)
- Leaderboard position

The player sits with their result. If they're a Seedling, they want to try again. If they're a Gardener, they want to reach Botanist. If they're a Master Gardener, they feel genuinely proud.

### Week 1: The Second and Third Run

The player returns for a second playthrough — this time choosing a different risk profile. The same events feel different through a Jungle lens versus a Zen lens. New plants unlock in different orders. The bad plants are no longer surprising, but managing them is still challenging.

By the third run, the player is making decisions instinctively. They hold through storms without hesitation. They diversify automatically. They avoid bad plants or use them strategically. **They've internalized the lessons.**

### Month 1: The Ambassador

The player recommends Growden to a friend. "It's this game where you build a garden, but it's actually about investing. Sounds weird, but trust me." They compare leaderboard scores. They debate whether Jungle or Zen is the better strategy.

The player has become a **financial literacy advocate** — not because someone told them to care, but because a garden made them care.

## 🎲 The Tension & Drama

The journey's dramatic arc follows a classic three-act structure:

- **Act 1 (Levels 1–3):** Discovery and delight. "This is fun and I'm learning."
- **Act 2 (Levels 4–7):** Complication and crisis. "This is harder than I thought."
- **Act 3 (Levels 8–10):** Resolution and mastery. "I know what I'm doing now."

The critical friction points — Junk Weed's default, Market Panic's temptation, Sector Mania's FOMO trap — are precisely placed to create memorable emotional peaks.

## 🧠 The Psychology

The journey exploits the **testing effect** — people remember information better when they're tested on it than when they study it. Every event is a test. Every decision is an answer. Every score is feedback. The player doesn't study investing. They practice it.

## 💰 The Business Connection

The player journey is designed around a **15-minute conversion window**. In that time, the player goes from "I don't understand investing" to "I want to learn more." PostFinance's call-to-action lives at the end of this journey — the debrief screen, the moment of reflection, the natural next question: "What if I did this for real?"

## ⚠️ Design Gaps & Open Questions

- ⚠️ **Design Gap** — *The journey ends abruptly after level 10. There's no "graduation ceremony" or bridge to real-world action. A PostFinance integration point (link to open an account, book an advisor, explore funds) would complete the conversion funnel.*
- ⚠️ **Design Gap** — *No re-engagement hooks between sessions. Push notifications, daily challenges, or streak rewards could drive return visits — but risk feeling manipulative for an educational product.*

## 🔮 Design Opportunities

1. **Post-Game Financial Profile:** Generate a personalized investing style report based on in-game decisions. "You're a patient, diversified investor with a weakness for high-yield temptations." Shareable, printable, and deeply personal.

2. **"What Would Have Happened" Mode:** After completing the game, show the player how their portfolio decisions would have played out with real historical market data. The garden metaphor meets financial reality.

3. **Onboarding to Real Investing:** Partner with PostFinance to create a seamless path from "Master Gardener" to "real account opened." The game becomes the top of the funnel for a financial services product.
