# Plants & Collection

> *Every plant is an asset class in disguise — and collecting them all means building the vocabulary of a real investor.*

---

## 🌟 The Fantasy

The player is a **botanical collector** building the most diverse, resilient garden in the world. Each new plant is a discovery — a new color, a new personality, a new way to survive the weather. Some plants are rare. Some are dangerous. All of them are teaching the player something about money.

The emotional pull isn't "I need more stocks." It's "I want that cherry blossom — and I want to see what it does in a thunderstorm."

## 🔄 The Loop

### Five Families, 25 Plants

Every plant in Growden belongs to one of five botanical families, each mapping to a real asset class:

**🌳 Trees = Equities (Stocks)**
Apple Tree (financial stocks), Cherry Blossom (tech), Magnolia (healthcare), Flame Tree (energy). Trees grow tall in sunshine and snap in storms. They're the garden's engine of growth — and its greatest source of drama.

**🌿 Bushes = Bonds**
Camellia (US Treasuries), Hydrangea (investment-grade), Hibiscus (high-yield), Bougainvillea (emerging markets). Bushes are steady and reassuring. They don't soar, but they rarely die. The player's safety net.

**🌱 Grass = Cash**
Meadow Grass (USD), Edelweiss (Swiss Franc), Clover (Euro), Silver Grass (Yen). Grass is boring — and that's the point. It barely moves. But during a rate hike, grass is the only thing growing. The game teaches that boring has value.

**🌵 Cacti = Commodities**
Golden Barrel (gold), Night-Blooming Cactus (oil), Prickly Pear (wheat), Cholla (copper). Cacti thrive in heat, drought, and chaos. When everything else is dying, the cactus stands tall. Players learn that real assets hedge against inflation.

**🌸 Orchids = Crypto**
White Phalaenopsis (Bitcoin), Purple Dendrobium (Ethereum), Blue Exotic Orchid (Solana), Green Cymbidium (stablecoins). Orchids are exotic, beautiful, and wildly volatile. They can double in a meteor shower — or lose 50% in a tornado. The player's high-risk, high-reward temptation.

### The Bad Plants: Lessons in Disguise

Three plants unlock in the later levels — and they're traps:

**🏭 Coal Bush (ESG Risk)** — Grows fast, pays well, and then gets hammered by regulatory events. Teaches that environmental and governance risk is financial risk.

**🌵 Junk Weed (Default Risk)** — High yield, but with a 4–35% chance of default depending on market conditions. Looks like a bond, crashes like a stock. Teaches that not all bonds are safe.

**🌿 Leveraged Vine (Liquidation Risk)** — Amplifies everything 5x. In a bull market, it's intoxicating. In a crash, it goes to zero. Permanently. The most visceral lesson in the game.

These bad plants are **never labeled as bad**. The player discovers the danger through experience — just like real investors discover that high yields come with high risks.

### Discovery and Unlocking

Plants unlock progressively across 10 levels:

- **Level 1:** The starter kit — one from each family. Just enough to learn the basics.
- **Levels 2–5:** The middle tier — specialized plants that react differently to specific events. The Cherry Blossom loves earnings season. The Golden Barrel loves chaos.
- **Levels 6–8:** The danger zone — bad plants appear alongside powerful defensive assets (Fortified Oak for defense stocks). The garden gets complicated.
- **Levels 9–10:** The full collection. The player has 25 options and must choose 10 for their garden. Every choice is a trade-off.

## 🎲 The Tension & Drama

The tension in the collection system comes from **constraint**. The player can only have 10 plants in their garden, but there are 25 to choose from. Every plant added means another left behind.

And each plant costs 100 flowers — the in-game currency earned through smart play. A player who panic-sold their way through early levels has fewer flowers, meaning fewer plants, meaning less diversification, meaning more vulnerability. **Past mistakes compound into future constraints.** Just like real investing.

The bad plants create a different kind of tension: **temptation**. Leveraged Vine's 5x returns are genuinely attractive — right up until the moment it liquidates. The emotional arc of "this plant is amazing → this plant destroyed me" is the game's most powerful teaching tool.

## 🧠 The Psychology

- **Collection Drive** — 25 plants with distinct names, emojis, and personalities. Players want to "catch 'em all." The Codex (plant encyclopedia) shows which plants they've unlocked and which remain hidden. Gaps beg to be filled.
- **Ownership Effect** — Once a player has invested flowers in a Cherry Blossom, they feel attached to it. Watching it wilt hurts. This emotional investment mirrors the endowment effect in real finance.
- **Categorization Pleasure** — Five families with clear visual identities. The human brain loves taxonomy. Sorting plants into trees/bushes/grass/cacti/orchids is inherently satisfying — and it's secretly teaching asset class categorization.
- **Narrative Identity** — Each plant has a Flora insight (educational flavor text) that tells its story. "The cactus was born for desert heat." These aren't data points — they're characters.

## 💰 The Business Connection

The plant collection system is the game's **primary content axis**. Each new plant is:

- A reason to play another level (unlock drive)
- A new strategic option (depth driver)
- A real-world concept learned (educational outcome)
- A potential monetization hook (exclusive plant skins, seasonal variants)

For PostFinance, the plant vocabulary becomes a **shared language** between the game and the bank's products. A player who understands why "bonds are bushes that protect the garden" is a customer who understands why a balanced fund includes fixed income.

## ⚠️ Design Gaps & Open Questions

- ⚠️ **Design Gap** — *All plants within a family react identically to events (all trees move together, all bushes move together). In reality, tech stocks and healthcare stocks behave very differently. The data architecture supports per-plant effects — is it worth the design complexity to implement them?*
- ⚠️ **Design Gap** — *Plant visual states (seed, sprout, growing, blooming) are based on portfolio value percentage, not time held. Should long-term holding unlock visual maturity? This would reinforce the "patience" lesson.*
- ⚠️ **Design Gap** — *There are no "rare" or "legendary" plants beyond the bad ones. A rarity system could deepen collection motivation — but risks overcomplicating the educational metaphor.*

## 🔮 Design Opportunities

1. **Seasonal Plant Variants:** Limited-time plant skins tied to real-world financial events. "2008 Oak" (grows back from nothing), "COVID Orchid" (extreme volatility skin). Collectibility meets financial history.

2. **Plant Evolution:** Plants that transform after surviving specific events. A Cherry Blossom that survives three storms becomes a "Tempered Cherry" with reduced volatility. This teaches that surviving crashes makes portfolios stronger.

3. **Plant Trading:** Let players trade plants with friends. "I'll give you my Flame Tree for your Golden Barrel." Social interaction meets portfolio theory meets the oldest game mechanic in the world.
