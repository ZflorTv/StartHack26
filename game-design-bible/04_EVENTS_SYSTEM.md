# Events System

> *Ten market events, three severity levels, infinite lessons — the weather engine that keeps every garden session feeling fresh and dangerous.*

---

## 🌟 The Fantasy

The player is watching the sky. They've built their garden, chosen their plants, and now the weather is coming. Will it be a gentle rain that nourishes the bonds? A scorching heatwave that empowers the cacti? Or a tornado that tests everything they've built?

The fantasy is **facing uncertainty with preparation**. The player doesn't control the weather — but they chose which plants to grow. Every event is a verdict on their decisions.

## 🔄 The Loop

### The Three Layers of Every Event

Every market event in Growden operates on three simultaneous layers:

**Layer 1: The Weather (What the player sees)**
Rain, sun, lightning, fog, tornado, meteor shower. These are visceral, visual, and immediately understandable. A tornado is scary before the player knows what it means financially.

**Layer 2: The Economic Trigger (What it represents)**
Unemployment spike, Fed rate hike, oil crisis, geopolitical shock. Each weather pattern maps to a real-world financial event. The player learns these connections through repetition — not through flashcards.

**Layer 3: The Amplitude (How bad is it?)**
Every event comes in three severities — Light, Moderate, and Severe. A light rain is a minor economic hiccup. A severe thunderstorm is a full-blown crisis. The same event at different amplitudes teaches that **magnitude matters** — a 0.25% rate hike and a 0.75% rate hike are fundamentally different beasts.

The amplitude multipliers are non-linear: Light events hit at 0.4x impact, Moderate at 1.0x, and Severe at a punishing 2.2x. This teaches **tail risk** — extreme events don't just hurt more, they hurt disproportionately more.

### The Ten Weather Events

| Event | Weather | Core Lesson |
|-------|---------|-------------|
| **Earnings Season** | ☀️ Sunshine | Stocks respond to surprises, not results |

### Signature Events

Two events deserve special attention for their design sophistication:

**The Calm Day ("Nothing Special")** — This is the event where *nothing happens*. Plants grow slowly. The sky is clear. And if the player trades during this calm period, they lose 10 points. The lesson: **most of investing is doing nothing**, and doing nothing well is the hardest skill to learn. The overtrading penalty is one of the game's most brilliant design choices.

**Sector Mania (Two-Phase Event)** — This is the game's masterpiece. Phase 1: crypto explodes +80%. The player feels euphoric. Their orchids are blooming like never before. Then the game offers a rebalance window — a chance to take profits. Phase 2: crypto crashes -75%. Players who held through the mania lose everything they gained and more. Players who rebalanced during the window keep their gains. **This single event teaches more about FOMO, bubble psychology, and rebalancing discipline than any textbook.**

## 🎲 The Tension & Drama

Events create drama through **asymmetric information**. The player can see the weather starting — rain clouds forming, lightning flickering — but they don't know the amplitude until it hits. Is this a light drizzle or a catastrophic flood?

This mirrors real markets perfectly. You can see a recession starting, but you don't know if it'll be mild or devastating until you're in it. The player's decisions during this uncertainty window — hold, rebalance, or panic — are the entire game.

### Escalating Severity

The game carefully controls event severity across levels:

- **Levels 1–3:** 60% Light events, only 10% Severe. Training wheels.
- **Levels 4–6:** Balanced distribution. The player faces real consequences.
- **Levels 7–9:** 50% Severe events. The garden is under siege.
- **Level 10:** 60% Severe. The final exam. Only well-diversified gardens survive.

## 🧠 The Psychology

- **Anticipation and Dread** — Seeing weather change before knowing the severity creates a suspense loop. The player scans their garden: "Am I ready for this?"
- **Pattern Recognition** — After several playthroughs, players start recognizing event-plant correlations. "Oil crisis incoming — good thing I have cacti." This is financial literacy being encoded as intuition.
- **Memorable Failure** — A player whose Leveraged Vine gets liquidated by a Severe Market Panic will never forget the lesson. Emotional experiences encode into long-term memory far more effectively than information.
- **FOMO Simulation** — The Sector Mania event creates genuine FOMO in a safe environment. Players feel the pull to hold through the mania — and learn why they shouldn't.

## 💰 The Business Connection

The events system is the game's **content engine** and **retention driver**:

- **10 events × 3 amplitudes = 30 unique scenarios** from a compact content investment
- **Level-gated event unlocking** gives players a reason to progress through the full campaign
- **Event unpredictability** ensures no two playthroughs feel identical, supporting replay value
- **Real-world mapping** means every event is a conversation starter: "I survived a Fed rate hike in Growden today" bridges gaming and financial literacy naturally

For PostFinance, events are also a **relevance engine**. When real-world events happen (an actual rate hike, an actual oil crisis), the game's existing content becomes immediately topical. Marketing can say: "The Fed just raised rates. Want to see how your garden would handle it?"

## ⚠️ Design Gaps & Open Questions

- ⚠️ **Design Gap** — *Events currently follow a fixed level-to-event mapping. Randomized event sequencing within levels would increase replayability — but risks undermining the carefully designed learning progression.*
- ⚠️ **Design Gap** — *Amplitude selection appears random within level-defined probability bands. Should player performance influence amplitude? Better portfolios face harder events? This creates adaptive difficulty but may feel punishing.*
- ⚠️ **Design Gap** — *No compound events (two events hitting simultaneously). Real markets face multiple shocks at once. A "Perfect Storm" mode could teach this — but may overwhelm new players.*

## 🔮 Design Opportunities

1. **Event Chains:** Linked events where the outcome of one influences the next. A rate hike followed by an unemployment spike (historically common) teaches players about economic causation, not just correlation.

2. **Player-Created Events:** Let advanced players design custom scenarios and challenge friends. "What if oil crashed and rates rose on the same day?" This turns the game into a financial sandbox.

3. **Historical Replay Mode:** Recreate famous market events (Black Monday, Dot-com Crash, 2008 Crisis, COVID Crash) as playable event sequences. History becomes a game level. Education becomes time travel.
